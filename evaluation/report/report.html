1. Methodology

1.1 Prompt Strategies

BASELINE (Naive)

Uses title only.

Example: "Coffee Mug"

No color, material, style, lighting, or background constraints.

STRUCTURED (Improved)

"Professional studio photography of a white ceramic Coffee Mug, minimalist aesthetic, centered composition, soft cinematic lighting, white solid background, 8k resolution, photorealistic, sharp focus."

1.2 Evaluation Setup

  • CLIP Score: openai/clip-vit-base-patch32 — measures semantic alignment between text prompt and generated image. Computed on seed-42 images.
  • SSIM: Pairwise Structural Similarity Index averaged across all 3 seeds (42, 123, 999) per product. Higher = more consistent visual structure.

3. Analysis & Key Findings

🎯

CLIP Alignment Preserved

Average CLIP decreased by only -0.009 (negligible). SDXL identifies the core object correctly regardless of prompt length — adding 25+ control tokens does not confuse subject recognition.

📐

Consistency Improved +22.8%

SSIM improved from 0.360 to 0.442 on average. Environmental anchoring (white background, cinematic lighting, centered composition) constrains the diffusion sampler's degrees of freedom.

🏆

Best Gains: Simple Geometry

Coffee Mug (+59%), Smart Speaker (+44%), and Sneakers (+105%) benefited most. Simple, smooth-surfaced objects respond best to structured prompts.

🧪

Material Tokens Effective

Keywords like "ceramic," "leather," and "mesh" visibly changed surface textures in outputs. Attribute-level control at the material domain is highly effective in SDXL.

4. Failure Cases & Limitations

📦 Leather Backpack — SSIM Regression

SSIM dropped from 0.614 → 0.258. The structured prompt activated different sub-concepts per seed (buckles vs. zippers vs. straps), creating structural divergence despite individually good images.

💡 Lesson: Multi-component products need explicit positional tokens (e.g., "single front pocket, no zippers")

👟 Running Shoes — Low SSIM Overall

Both baseline (0.100) and improved (0.177) remain low. Footwear with laces, mesh, and sole geometry has high natural variance across seeds.

💡 Lesson: ControlNet or img2img conditioning required for geometric precision

⌚ Wrist Watch — Fine Detail Loss

Dial numbers and watch hands are inconsistent. SDXL's 1024×1024 resolution is insufficient for sub-millimetre rendering of fine mechanical details.

💡 Lesson: Super-resolution post-processing (ESRGAN) needed for accessory detail

📊 Running Shoes — Low Baseline

The simple title "Running Shoes" is ambiguous — the model generates everything from high-tops to low-profile, various color schemes, and different viewing angles per seed.

💡 Lesson: Even structured prompts cannot fully compensate for inherent model variance on complex objects

⚖️ Key Trade-off: Control vs. Diversity

Higher SSIM (consistency) naturally reduces creative variety. For e-commerce catalogs this is a desirable trade-off — brand consistency matters more than diversity. For artistic generation, baseline prompts produce more interesting variation.

5. Conclusions

  • ✅ Structured prompt engineering measurably improves cross-seed consistency (+22.8% SSIM) for e-commerce images.
  • ✅ CLIP alignment is preserved — adding control tokens does not degrade object identification.
  • ✅ Pipeline is fully automated and reproducible — 60 images generated from CSV metadata via one-click Colab execution.
  • ⚠️ Products with complex geometry (footwear, multi-pocket bags) require additional control mechanisms beyond text prompting.
  • 🚀 Future work: ControlNet for pose/angle control; real Amazon metadata for 100+ product scalability test; ESRGAN super-resolution for accessory detail.
